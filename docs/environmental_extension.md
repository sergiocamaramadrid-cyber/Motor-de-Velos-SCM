Environmental Extension — SCM Motor de Velos

Status

Working hypothesis integrated into the framework.
Not a confirmed result.
Designed as a falsifiable environmental test.

---

1. Motivation

The SCM framework studies the structure of the outer region of galaxy rotation curves through the observable:

[
F3_{SCM} = \frac{d\log V}{d\log r}
]

measured in the external region of the rotation curve.

The environmental extension explores whether the external cosmic environment can produce a secondary modulation of the outer slope beyond the internal baryonic structure.

The base idea is conservative:

outer rotation behaviour = internal baryonic response + environmental modulation

The baryonic mass remains the primary driver, but the surrounding medium could introduce a small systematic perturbation.

---

2. Observable

The preferred observable for the environmental test is:

[
\Delta F3 = F3_{SCM} - 0.5
]

Reason:

• centers the observable
• measures deviation from the reference regime
• easier interpretation of sign
• better suited for regression tests

Interpretation:

ΔF3 &gt; 0 → outer slope more rising than expected
ΔF3 ≈ 0 → consistent with reference regime
ΔF3 &lt; 0 → flatter or declining outer region

---

3. Environmental Hypothesis

At approximately fixed baryonic mass and internal structure, galaxies embedded in denser or dynamically more constrained environments may show systematically different outer slopes compared to galaxies in low-density environments.

Formally:

[
\Delta F3 \sim f(\rho_{env})
]

where:

[
\rho_{env}
]

is a proxy for the large-scale environment.

---

4. Environmental Proxies

The framework prioritizes observable and reproducible proxies.

Level 1 (operational)

Σ5 — projected density from the 5th nearest neighbor
distance to filament
distance to cluster / node
void / field / group / cluster classification

Level 2 (extended dynamics)

local density gradient
anisotropic environment
matter inflow toward nodes

Level 3 (conceptual only)

extreme cosmic structures such as very large walls.

These are kept as conceptual motivation but not used as operational tests.

---

5. Testable Prediction

The environmental hypothesis predicts:

[
\Delta F3_{dense} &lt; \Delta F3_{void}
]

Meaning:

galaxies in dense environments should show slightly flatter outer slopes than comparable galaxies in low-density environments.

---

6. Statistical Strategy

The environmental test must control for internal properties.

Recommended regression:

[
\Delta F3 \sim environment + mass + size + inclination
]

Robustness tests:

matched galaxy pairs with similar baryonic mass
different environmental location
out-of-sample validation
AICc comparison between models
coefficient sign stability

---

7. Relation to the SCM framework

This extension connects directly with the concept of flow dynamics and node gradients previously identified in the SCM framework.

The environment may affect the external region of galaxies through:

• matter inflow along filaments
• proximity to cluster potential wells
• anisotropic large-scale gravitational fields

These effects are expected to be secondary, not dominant.

---

8. Scientific Scope

The environmental extension is intended to:

test whether a residual environmental signal exists in the outer slope observable.

It does not claim:

• that environment dominates galaxy rotation curves
• that large cosmic structures determine galactic dynamics
• that the SCM framework requires environmental terms to function.

The extension simply tests whether environment leaves a measurable imprint.

---

9. Future Extensions

Future work may incorporate:

HI outer surface density
filament orientation relative to galaxy disk
velocity flow toward nodes
higher redshift rotation curves from upcoming surveys.

---

10. Summary

The SCM environmental extension proposes that the outer slope observable ΔF3 may contain a secondary dependence on the cosmic environment.

This prediction is falsifiable and testable using existing rotation curve catalogues combined with environmental surveys.

The environmental signal, if present, would appear as a small systematic trend rather than a dominant dynamical driver.
