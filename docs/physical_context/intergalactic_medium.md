# Intergalactic Medium and Environmental Context

## Overview

The large-scale universe is not empty. Galaxies are embedded in a multi-component medium consisting of radiation, ionized gas, relic particles and large-scale gravitational structures. These components define the physical environment in which galaxy dynamics evolve.

Understanding this environment is essential when testing hypotheses about the origin of galaxy rotation curves and possible environmental contributions to galactic dynamics.

---

## Composition of Intergalactic Space

The intergalactic space contains several well-characterized components:

| Component | Typical Density |
|-----------|----------------|
| Cosmic Microwave Background | ~5×10⁻³⁴ g/cm³ |
| Intergalactic Medium (IGM) | 10⁻²⁹ – 10⁻²⁷ g/cm³ |
| Cosmic Neutrino Background | ~336 cm⁻³ |
| Galaxies | <10⁻⁵ of total volume |

The CMB provides a uniform radiation background with temperature:

T = 2.725 K

Photon number density:

n ≈ 410 cm⁻³

---

## Intergalactic Medium Properties

The intergalactic medium consists mainly of ionized hydrogen and helium distributed along the cosmic web.

Typical densities:

ρ ≈ 10⁻²⁹ – 10⁻²⁷ g/cm³

Typical particle densities:

n ≈ 1–100 atoms/m³

The thermal pressure of the IGM can be approximated by:

P = n k_B T

Typical value today:

P_IGM ≈ 10⁻¹⁵ Pa

---

## Large-Scale Structure

Galaxies form along filaments of the cosmic web where matter density is enhanced.

Typical peculiar velocities along filaments:

v ≈ 200–600 km/s

Voids represent low-density regions where expansion rates can exceed the cosmic mean.

---

## Hydrodynamic Framework

If the surrounding medium contributes dynamically to galaxy rotation, the radial acceleration may be expressed as:

v² / r = GM_bar / r² + (1/ρ) (dP/dr)

Where:

v = rotation velocity  
r = galactocentric radius  
M_bar = baryonic mass  
ρ = density of surrounding medium  
P = pressure

The second term represents a possible contribution from pressure gradients in the surrounding medium.

---

## Observational Test

The SCM Framework tests whether galaxy dynamics correlate with environmental quantities.

Two observables are used:

ΔF3  
β

Defined as:

ΔF3 = d(log V_obs) / d(log r)

β = d(log g_obs) / d(log g_bar)

The framework tests correlations between these observables and environmental proxies such as:

log Σ_HI,out  
log M_bar  
log R_d
